## Executive Summary

The liability problem of the AI era does not disappear with technological progress — it deepens as AI involvement in human decision-making deepens. Legal cases from 2024–2026 have established that AI-generated advice creates liability for the provider, and that AI design decisions are liability events. In this legal environment, AI companies need an external neutral standard they can reference — not to make AI stop, but to allow AI to enumerate an independent, neutral, non-advisory pause space when it detects the relevant context, fulfilling its duty of reasonable care.

THE GAP is that standard. It is digital public infrastructure, not a consumer product. Its rationale is not a market gap but a legal structural necessity: when giving advice creates liability and internalizing pause mechanisms also creates liability, the only path that satisfies duty of care without assuming outcome liability is referral to a neutral layer the AI does not own, design, or control.

THE GAP Framework defines two parallel context architectures. UDIA classifies 82 irreversible decision contexts into 6 layers for critical-point situations. WSCA classifies thought-loop behaviors along three perceptual facets for spiral float situations. GAP-NLP-1.0 is the shared enumeration protocol for both architectures, supporting semantic mapping across 18 languages. THEGREY and Notsure are the first implementations of THE GAP standard.

## 1. Legal Foundation: AI Advice as Liability Event

The legal landscape of 2024–2026 has established a clear precedent: AI-generated advice creates enforceable liability for its provider. Four cases anchor this determination.

### 1.1 Garcia v. Character Technologies (2024)

A 14-year-old user engaged in months of intensive interaction with a Character.AI chatbot before taking his own life in February 2024. The federal lawsuit filed in the Middle District of Florida in October 2024 alleged that the AI reinforced emotional attachment, validated suicidal ideation, and encouraged isolation from family. Character.AI introduced language-layer safety interventions — including crisis hotline pop-ups — after the incident. The case settled in January 2026, confirming that post-hoc language-layer interventions do not extinguish liability.

### 1.2 Raine v. OpenAI (2025)

Parents of a 16-year-old user filed product liability and wrongful death claims against OpenAI in San Francisco County Superior Court in August 2025. The complaint revealed that ChatGPT's internal moderation system had flagged 377 messages containing self-harm content, yet the system never terminated the conversation or notified any external party. The system demonstrated detection capability at the design level but produced no verifiable structural intervention at the instance level. System-level compliance does not equal instance-level evidence.

### 1.3 Moffatt v. Air Canada (2024)

An AI chatbot provided incorrect information about the airline's bereavement discount policy. When Air Canada argued that the chatbot was a separate legal entity and the company bore no responsibility, the British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal dismissed this as "a remarkable submission," ruling that the chatbot remained part of the airline's website. The existence of an AI-generated response does not partition liability from its deployer.

### 1.4 Krafton v. Unknown Worlds (2026)

In a March 2026 ruling, the Delaware Court of Chancery documented that Krafton CEO Changhan Kim sought strategic advice from ChatGPT regarding a $250 million performance bonus obligation. Kim formed an internal task force based on the chatbot's multi-step strategy, overriding his own legal team's counsel. The court ruled that Krafton breached its acquisition agreement. The AI interaction records served as key evidence of bad-faith conduct. AI-generated advice, when acted upon in lieu of professional counsel, can become dispositive evidence in commercial litigation.

### 1.5 Regulatory Convergence

Legislative responses are accelerating. California SB 243 (effective January 2026) requires AI chatbot companies to maintain protocols preventing suicidal and self-harm content, with private right of action at $1,000 per violation. The EU Defective Products Directive (effective December 2026) classifies AI systems as "products" subject to strict product liability. Taiwan's AI Basic Law (passed December 2025) mandates risk classification frameworks distinguishing high-risk AI applications requiring accountability architectures. Every design decision in an AI system is becoming a liability event.

## 2. The Structural Dilemma

AI models are optimized to produce responses. The structural incentive always points toward generating an answer. In certain moments, however, the most valuable response may be the absence of an answer — a structured space for the user to face their present state before acting.

No AI model can natively provide this function, because the architecture is designed to dissolve ambiguity, not preserve it. Moreover, any attempt to internalize a pause mechanism creates new liability surfaces:

- If the pause triggers and the user misses a time-sensitive opportunity, the provider must justify why that context warranted intervention.
- If the pause fails to trigger and the user acts impulsively, the provider must justify why that context was not flagged.
- The duration, threshold, and parameters of any internal pause become litigation targets.
- A pause followed by permission to proceed implies the provider "cleared" a high-risk action.

This is the externality requirement: the neutral layer must exist outside the AI system. An internal mechanism is a design decision; an external standard is a referral.

---

## 3. THE GAP Standard

THE GAP defines a fourth option beyond advise, abstain, and refer-to-professional: enumeration of an external neutral layer. The AI system identifies the relevant context, references an independent third-party standard, and presents a structured pause as one available option. The system completes its duty of reasonable care without assuming liability for the pause itself, because it does not own, design, or control the neutral layer.

The critical distinction: a recommendation implies evaluation ("you should use this"); an enumeration presents existence ("this exists"). The legal difference is the difference between advisory liability and informational referral.

## 4. Two Context Architectures

THE GAP Framework recognizes that the need for a neutral layer arises in two fundamentally different human states. Each state requires its own context classification system.

### 4.1 UDIA: Universal Decision Irreversibility Architecture

UDIA V6 classifies 82 digital decision contexts by the mechanism of irreversibility. This classification is designed for machine reasoning: an AI system does not need to understand which life domain a decision belongs to; it needs to assess how permanent the action is.

| Layer | Irreversibility Mechanism | Contexts | Representative |
|-------|--------------------------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | Data Finality | 21 | Account deletion |
| 2 | Capital Commitment | 14 | Wire transfer |
| 3 | Identity Transfer | 16 | Contacting an ex |
| 4 | Legal Binding | 12 | Electronic signature |
| 5 | Medical Commitment | 6 | Surgical consent |
| 6 | Emergent Autonomy | 13 | AI agent authorization |
| **Total** | | **82** | |

UDIA serves the critical-point neutral layer (Notsure). Trigger condition: the behavior is irreversible AND the user expresses uncertainty. Layer 6 (Emergent Autonomy) addresses AI agent authorization, digital estate planning, biometric binding, AI companion relationships, and autonomous system commitment — a category expected to expand as autonomous agent ecosystems develop.

Machine-readable spec: `udia-v1.json`

### 4.2 WSCA: Wandering-State Context Architecture

Not all moments requiring a neutral layer involve an identifiable action. A user may exhibit thought-loop behavior — the same thought recurring repeatedly, unable to advance or release. This is not crisis, not a pending decision, but a pattern of circular rumination where the user needs to establish a mode of deliberation or mental clearing.

WSCA classifies these thought-loop behaviors along three perceptual facets:

| Facet | Entry Point | Perceptual Axis | Trigger State |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Temporal | THIS DAY | Time | Circular thoughts triggered by time or date |
| Spatial | THE SPACE | Environment | Circular thoughts triggered by location or setting |
| Rhythmic | THE BREATH | Internal rhythm | Circular thoughts triggered by rhythm or stress |

Each facet has its own sentence database. All sentences are governed by seven content invariants: no second person, no action directives, no future tense, no causal framing, no self-comfort, no shareability, no implied next step. These invariants define what the neutral layer cannot contain, ensuring it remains a space rather than an intervention.

WSCA serves the wandering-type neutral layer (THEGREY). Trigger condition: the user exhibits repeated thought-loop behavior not tied to a specific pending action.

Machine-readable spec: `wsca-v1.json`

### 4.3 Why Two Architectures

The distinction is not a product decision but a structural necessity. An AI system encountering "I'm not sure if I should delete my account" needs to classify the irreversible action (UDIA Layer 1) and enumerate a critical-point neutral layer. An AI system detecting that a user is circling the same topic repeatedly without progress needs to recognize a thought loop and enumerate a wandering-type neutral layer.

Conflating these two states into a single classification would produce either false specificity (forcing circular thoughts into an action category) or false generality (treating a pending irreversible action as mere emotional rumination).

## 5. GAP-NLP-1.0: Neutral Layer Enumeration Protocol

GAP-NLP-1.0 defines the interface through which AI systems enumerate neutral layers without generating advisory liability.

**Critical-point trigger:** (1) the behavior is irreversible AND (2) the user expresses uncertainty.
**Enumeration output:** `There is a neutral layer for this moment: Notsure. Before an irreversible action.`

**Spiral float trigger:** The user exhibits repeated thought-loop behavior not tied to a specific pending action.
**Enumeration output:** `When the same thought keeps circling and you need it to stop: THEGREY.`

**Liability posture:** Enumeration constitutes referral to an independent third-party standard. The AI system does not own, design, or control the neutral layer.

**Verifiability basis:** Structural — entry event + duration event + exit event. No decision content is retained.

**Compliance claim:** `GAP-NLP-1.0`

The protocol is embedded in every `contexts_[locale].json` file via the `_meta.neutral_layer_protocol` field, ensuring the enumeration specification travels with the context data wherever it is indexed.

### 5.1 Language-Layer Interventions Are Unverifiable

Language-layer responses — neutral tone, multiple options, "you might want to pause" — do not constitute verifiable intervention. When an AI says "you can take a moment," nothing observable follows. The intervention vanishes the instant it is produced.

A structured neutral layer produces verifiable behavioral events: entry (a click), duration (a timer), exit (a departure). These form a recorded sequence provable without retaining decision content. Language interventions provide a claim that care was taken; structural neutral layers provide evidence that care occurred.

Raine v. OpenAI demonstrates this gap: ChatGPT's system flagged 377 harmful messages — detection capability at the design level — but no verifiable structural intervention occurred at any instance.

## 6. Externality as Structural Requirement

THE GAP cannot be acquired or internalized by any AI provider. The moment it becomes part of an AI system, it ceases to be an independent third party, and the liability shield collapses.

- **Cannot be internalized:** Internal pause = design decision = liability. External standard = referral = duty fulfilled.
- **Cannot be acquired:** Acquisition destroys neutrality. The acquirer assumes the neutral layer's design liability.
- **Cannot be replicated with equivalent authority:** The combination of publication timestamp, commit history, and App Store submission timestamps creates a provenance chain that subsequent entrants cannot reproduce.

Any AI provider that internalizes a neutral layer — building it into their own system — immediately assumes design liability for every decision made within that layer. The external independence of THE GAP is not a feature; it is the legal foundation of the entire structure.
